# Voice & Style Guide — Lupine Field Coffee

## Brand Voice

**Voice adjectives:** declarative, grounded, dry, journalistic, generous

**If this brand were a person, they would be:** A thoughtful longtime journalist who left newsrooms to build something with their hands. Reads widely. Trusts the reader to follow a complete sentence. Doesn't try to sound smart but is.

## Tone by Context

| Context | Tone |
|---|---|
| Homepage hero | Declarative, single line. "Coffee with a paper trail." Not "Discover the difference." |
| Product descriptions | Diary-entry honest. State what the coffee is, where it came from, what it tastes like in plain English. |
| Error messages / 404 | Calm and slightly dry. "That page is not here. Maybe try [link]?" Not cute, not panicked. |
| Email subject lines | Specific and quiet. "New from Yirgacheffe." Not "🔥 NEW DROP YOU WON'T BELIEVE." |
| Cart / checkout | Functional and brief. No upsell shouting. |
| About page | Personal, narrative, complete sentences. The longest copy on the site lives here, and it should. |

## Writing Rules

### Always Do
- Write declarative sentences. Subject, verb, object.
- Use specific numbers ("$4.85/lb green") instead of generalities ("fair pricing").
- Trust the reader to know what an espresso is, what a co-op is, what natural processing is.
- Use the Oxford comma.
- Cite sources when making a factual claim.

### Never Do
- Use sale urgency language ("Only 3 left!" "While supplies last!" — except the literal phrase "Available while the lot lasts" which is fine on PDP).
- Use exclamation points anywhere on the site. Ever.
- Use the word "premium," "artisan," "craft," "curated," "hand-selected," "elevated," or "experience" (as a noun).
- Use wine-tasting note theatrics ("hints of jasmine and stone fruit with a buttery finish"). Describe what it actually tastes like.
- Use the word "we believe" — show the belief through the action, don't announce it.
- Write subheaders that are full sentences. Subheaders are noun phrases.

## Vocabulary

**Preferred terms:**
| Instead of... | Use... |
|---|---|
| Premium / artisan / craft | (just describe the actual thing — origin, process, varietal) |
| Hand-selected | Bought from |
| Curated | Chosen |
| Roastery experience | The roastery |
| Sourcing partners | Producers (or the co-op name) |
| Customers | Subscribers, drinkers, or just "people" |
| Discover | See, read, try |
| Journey | (delete — never used) |
| Notes of [X] | Tastes like [X] (and only when honestly true) |

## Sentence Style

**Sentence length:** Mixed. Short sentences for emphasis. Longer compound sentences for narrative passages on the About page and product descriptions. Avoid the staccato "every. sentence. is. four. words." rhythm.
**Use of contractions:** Yes. Always. ("don't" not "do not")
**Use of exclamation points:** Never. Not even in casual contexts.
**Oxford comma:** Yes.
**Numbers:** Numerals always for measurements and prices. Spell out numbers under 10 in narrative prose ("we visited four farms last year").

## Sample Copy (Good)

**From the homepage:**
> Most coffee brands talk about "fair sourcing" without ever showing you a number. We do it differently. Every bag we sell has the green coffee price we paid, the date we bought it, and the markup that pays our roaster, our staff, and the lights.

**From a product page (Yirgacheffe):**
> A washed Ethiopian from the Konga cooperative, in the Yirgacheffe zone of southern Ethiopia. We bought 660 lbs in October — enough to last about three months at our current roast volume. Konga has been working with washed processing for decades; what we get is bright, almost floral, with a clean finish. Brew it as a pour-over with water just off the boil. We paid $4.85/lb green.

**From an email subject line:**
> New from Yirgacheffe. Subscribers see it first.

## Sample Copy (Bad — what to avoid)

> ✨ DISCOVER our PREMIUM, hand-selected single-origin Ethiopian Yirgacheffe! 🔥 Featuring delicate notes of jasmine and stone fruit with a buttery finish, this artisan-roasted coffee is the perfect way to elevate your morning routine. Limited stock — get yours before it's gone! ☕

(Every single thing wrong with this brief is wrong with that sentence.)
